preface

As databanks keep growing, the layman no longer knows "where to sit". Databases won’t be limited to professionals, but will become an integral part of general consumers’ lives. At the start of a search, the user will express a certain interest, which may later be specified and corrected interactively. What they want to know is then automatically selected and ideally ordered. This implies, of course, that when entering data into a data repository, an additional structure must be woven in, enabling selection and compression.

This is very much the case today with algorithms as massive search engines would be unusable without them, or atleast extremely difficult.

Algorithms often feel invisible, you don’t notice them before they’re gone. I realized this, when I switched between music streaming platforms. Often listening to music on the go, you want to find an album you’re looking for fast and frictionless. When I started using a smaller platform instead of Spotify, I noticed how looking up songs, albums or artists became more tedious, as you had to write out titles and names in full length to get relevant results.

Spotify uses a neural architecture which they developed, called Neural Instant Search. Very briefly, it improves the search process by updating results in real-time with every keystroke, using character-level embedding. Along with sophisticated personalization and user profiling, this results in a more efficient experience compared to smaller platforms. Of course, this architecture is much more complicated than that, about which you can read from the following paper

Coming back to my personal experience, although unsubscribing from Spotify was just a sacrifice of comfort, it made me notice how efficient their search algorithm was. We're living in a world where algorithms silently shape our experiences, from what songs we listen to, to what news we read. Thus it’s important to be aware and ideally even understand them.

Birthday cards sometimes congratulate and sing, and there are car dashboards that provide speaking warnings. It is proving to be a tough technical and linguistic task, however, to strip the human, free flow of sounds of noise and convert them into words and sentences, which are also spelled and classified correctly. However, talking to a printer, or having a printout of a phone conversation readily available, is too fascinating not to be explored and developed. The path from printed text to clear and understandable speech synthesis has other problems, such as rhythm and intonation, which in fact presuppose an intelligent interpretation of the message. Should these then be precoded? Speech synthesis could be a solution for the blind and visually impaired, of whom in fact only a minority can read Braille. However, a much wider use is conceivable.

Aside from sorting and recommendation, algorithms also played an essential role in the evolution of speech synthesis. Although the first speech synthesis systems had already been invented even before the publication of this book, different algorithms and machine learning have vastly improved it. There have been major advances in the pronunciation, pace and expressiveness which make speech synthesis sound more natural and human-like.

One important thing is that modern text-to-speech systems are better at using resources, which means they can work in real-time on many different devices, from powerful computers to smartphones and even small gadgets. This makes speech synthesis useful in more situations.

Additionally, modern systems can work with many languages and dialects, which makes them useful for a wider audience all around the world.

In web environments, speech synthesis is used by screen readers to help people who can't see the screen. This is why it's important for web designers and developers to follow the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG), making websites accessible to everyone. Some key things WCAG asks for are providing text descriptions or captions for things like images, audio, and videos. This helps screen readers tell people what's in these things. To do this, you simply need to add a short description for images in your website's code.

WCAG also emphasizes that websites should be easy to use with just a keyboard, which is important for people who can't use a mouse. This means that website designers and developers need to pay attention to features such as keyboard shortcuts, logical tab orders, and providing visible focus indicators for interactive elements. These considerations make websites not only more accessible but also more user-friendly for a diverse range of users who may have different abilities and needs.
